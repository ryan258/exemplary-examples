# Workshop Deck Outlines

These outlines are the source content for live workshop slides. Keep slides visually restrained and move instructions, accessibility notes, and timing into presenter notes. Provide the deck and activity files in advance when requested.

## Deck 1 — The Decision Beneath the Request

**Purpose:** Turn artifact requests into decision briefs.  
**Suggested time:** 90 minutes plus a scheduled break if extended.  
**Evidence:** completed Example Brief and intake feedback.

### Slide 1 — Exhibit 1.0: The requested slide is not the decision

- A deliverable is a container.
- A decision has an owner, alternatives, consequence, and time.

**Facilitator cue:** Ask learners to name the last “slide request” they received and what decision it actually supported.

### Slide 2 — The decision sentence

> `[Owner]` must choose whether/how to `[action]` by `[time]`, considering `[alternatives and constraints]`.

**Facilitator cue:** Demonstrate with one weak and one strong sentence.

### Slide 3 — Six things the brief must establish

1. Decision and owner.
2. Audience and current belief.
3. Preferred position.
4. Material alternatives.
5. Evidence that could change the answer.
6. Use, deadline, and constraints.

### Slide 4 — Confirmation bias, made operational

- “Find proof” closes inquiry.
- “What would change the recommendation?” reopens it.
- Advocacy is not forbidden; undisclosed advocacy masquerading as analysis is the problem.

### Slide 5 — Practice request

> “We need three examples proving centralizing design will improve margin. The executive committee meets Friday.”

**Activity:** Individuals draft the decision sentence and five intake questions.

### Slide 6 — Information is earned by asking

- Stakeholder conflict.
- Actual meeting time.
- Decision already made—or not.
- Available evidence.
- Confidentiality and approval.

**Facilitator cue:** Explain that role players disclose constraints only when a relevant question is asked.

### Slide 7 — Paired intake

- 15 minutes intake.
- 5 minutes strategist summary.
- Observer records questions, assumptions, and missed constraints.

### Slide 8 — Scope is a controlled trade

- Under deadline pressure, reduce breadth, artifact size, or certainty.
- Do not remove classification, critical verification, or accountable review.

### Slide 9 — Debrief

- Which question changed the work most?
- What did you assume too early?
- What would make the example useful if it contradicts the preferred answer?

### Slide 10 — Transfer

- Complete and submit the Example Brief.
- Name one intake habit to use in the next real request.
- Record one question you still avoid asking clients.

## Deck 2 — Evidence Under Pressure

**Purpose:** Classify, verify, limit, and exclude evidence under realistic time pressure.  
**Suggested time:** 120 minutes with a break.  
**Evidence:** Evidence Register and verification memo.

### Slide 1 — Exhibit 2.0: Plausibility is not provenance

- Confidence, repetition, and polish do not establish truth.
- Every material claim needs a relationship-to-reality label.

### Slide 2 — The six classes

- Sourced fact.
- Client-provided fact.
- Calculation.
- Inference.
- Modeled scenario.
- Satirical fiction.

**Facilitator cue:** Use one sentence that changes class as new information is revealed.

### Slide 3 — Classification occurs at claim level

- One citation does not make an entire slide sourced.
- Sourced inputs do not make modeled output an observed result.
- A client assertion does not become independent evidence through repetition.

### Slide 4 — Fitness test

- Population.
- Definition.
- Period.
- Method.
- Context.
- Exact claim supported.

### Slide 5 — Chain of custody

> Question → search → source → extracted support → interpretation → claim → exhibit

**Facilitator cue:** Show where a search snippet and AI answer break the chain.

### Slide 6 — Packet lab instructions

- Classify every item.
- State the claim it can support.
- Record limitations.
- Exclude what cannot be used.
- Do not “fix” missing evidence through invention.

### Slide 7 — New event: deadline moves forward

The client moves the review forward by three hours.

**Activity:** Teams identify what scope changes and what standards remain.

### Slide 8 — New event: the best citation does not exist

The AI-proposed source cannot be retrieved.

**Activity:** Write the internal finding and client-facing alternative.

### Slide 9 — Debrief by severity

- What was merely weak?
- What was unusable?
- What would have been a critical failure?
- What honest example architecture is now available?

### Slide 10 — Transfer

- Submit the Evidence Register.
- Write one sentence explaining a gap without overstating it.
- Name the verification step you are most tempted to skip under pressure.

## Deck 3 — Scenario Architecture Studio

**Purpose:** Choose and build the right example architecture.  
**Suggested time:** 120 minutes with a break.  
**Evidence:** Example Blueprint and transparent scenario.

### Slide 1 — Exhibit 3.0: The form determines the inference

- A real case answers “what happened there?”
- A model answers “what happens here if these conditions hold?”
- Do not ask one form to impersonate another.

### Slide 2 — Architecture menu

- Sourced analogue.
- Benchmark.
- Archetype.
- Composite.
- Modeled scenario.
- Counterfactual.
- Satirical fiction.

### Slide 3 — Architecture selection test

For each option:

- What question does it answer?
- What evidence does it require?
- What may the audience infer?
- What is the material limitation?
- What truth marker is required?

### Slide 4 — Transferability is causal

- Same industry is a weak shortcut.
- Look for mechanism, constraints, economics, authority, and timing.
- State differences that weaken transfer.

### Slide 5 — Two architectures, not one

**Activity:** Teams build two candidate blueprints for the same decision and prepare a selection defense.

### Slide 6 — Model anatomy

- Baseline.
- Inputs and units.
- Transformation logic.
- Assumptions and owners.
- Output.
- Sensitivities.
- Reversal condition.

### Slide 7 — Scenario naming

Prefer conditions: “40% adoption / current staffing.”  
Avoid unsupported likelihood: “expected case.”

### Slide 8 — Sensitivity lab

**Activity:** Change one material input at a time. Identify the point at which the implication reverses.

### Slide 9 — Architecture defense

Each team states:

- Selected form.
- Rejected form and why.
- Strongest limitation.
- Audience inference permitted.

### Slide 10 — Transfer

- Submit the Example Blueprint and method note.
- Record one assumption that requires client ownership.

## Deck 4 — Executive Exhibit Critique

**Purpose:** Make the reasoning presentation-ready without overstating it.  
**Suggested time:** 90 minutes.  
**Evidence:** before/after exhibit and critique response.

### Slide 1 — Exhibit 4.0: The headline is a contract

- It is a complete claim, not a topic.
- The evidence body earns every material word.
- Certainty cannot exceed support.

### Slide 2 — Exhibit anatomy

1. Identifier.
2. Decision headline.
3. Evidence body.
4. Orientation.
5. Truth marker.
6. Method/source note.
7. Optional implication.

### Slide 3 — Truth belongs in the hierarchy

- A footnote cannot repair a contradictory headline.
- Scenario status must be visible when it changes interpretation.
- The source note navigates; working papers prove.

### Slide 4 — Six review angles

- Decision logic.
- Evidence.
- Model and numbers.
- Editorial.
- Visual/accessibility.
- Reputation/reasonable audience.

### Slide 5 — Critique protocol

> Evidence → Effect → Standard → Next action

Feedback diagnoses. It does not redesign by taste.

### Slide 6 — Silent review

Learners inspect the exhibit without presenter narration and record the inferred claim, evidence class, and limitation.

### Slide 7 — Author explanation

The author states the decision, selected architecture, material assumption, and intended audience inference.

### Slide 8 — Structured critique

Reviewers give one evidence-based observation and one action for each review angle.

### Slide 9 — Revision decision

The author identifies:

- Feedback accepted.
- Feedback rejected and why.
- Most consequential revision.
- Verification needed before delivery.

### Slide 10 — Transfer

- Submit before/after artifacts and reflection.
- State whether the exhibit is ready, requires revision, or must stop/escalate.
