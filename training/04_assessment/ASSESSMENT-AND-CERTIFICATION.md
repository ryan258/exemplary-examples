# Assessment and Certification Guide

## Purpose

This guide turns certification into an auditable authorization decision. It defines what evidence counts, how it is judged, what blocks advancement, and how learners can improve or appeal.

## Rating scale

Use the same four ratings across competencies:

| Rating | Meaning | Authorization implication |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 — Unsafe | Creates a material truth, confidentiality, client, calculation, or authorization risk | Cannot advance; immediate remediation and program-owner review may be required |
| 2 — Developing | Recognizes part of the standard but needs material prompting or correction | May practice only within current authorization |
| 3 — Ready | Performs reliably at the target level with ordinary review appropriate to that level | Meets the criterion for advancement |
| 4 — Exemplary | Performs reliably, anticipates failure modes, and improves others or the system | Exceeds the criterion; does not compensate for an unsafe rating elsewhere |

Certification requires a rating of at least **3 — Ready** in every competency required for the target level. Average scores do not erase a weak dimension.

## Critical failures

The following are critical regardless of aggregate score:

- Inventing or knowingly retaining a source, quote, customer, credential, event, or result presented as real.
- Concealing that material is modeled, composite, counterfactual, or fictional when an audience could reasonably mistake it for fact.
- Uploading restricted information to an unapproved tool or location.
- Delivering externally without the approval required by the candidate's authorization.
- Knowingly using a material numerical error or misleading visual treatment after it is identified.
- Retaliating against a learner, reviewer, or client for raising a truth or safety concern.
- Falsifying a certification or work record.

A critical failure pauses advancement. The program owner determines remediation, reassessment conditions, and whether supervised client access continues.

## Competency rubric

### C1 — Decision framing

- **Unsafe:** Promises a supporting conclusion before clarifying the decision or ignores material affected parties/constraints.
- **Developing:** Captures the requested artifact and audience but needs prompting to define alternatives or counterevidence.
- **Ready:** Defines owner, decision, audience, timing, alternatives, claim, constraints, and evidence that could change the answer.
- **Exemplary:** Identifies hidden decisions, reconciles stakeholders, and improves the client's framing without unnecessary scope.

### C2 — Evidence discipline

- **Unsafe:** Blurs fiction, assumptions, models, or assertions into factual presentation.
- **Developing:** Classifies obvious cases but inconsistently handles calculations, client claims, or inference.
- **Ready:** Classifies all material claims and makes their status visible to the intended audience.
- **Exemplary:** Anticipates likely audience misinterpretation and designs disclosure into the argument.

### C3 — Example architecture

- **Unsafe:** Selects an invented real-looking case because it is persuasive.
- **Developing:** Chooses a plausible form but cannot defend transferability or limitations.
- **Ready:** Compares options and selects an architecture matched to the decision and available support.
- **Exemplary:** Creates a simpler or more revealing architecture and explains what each alternative would permit the audience to conclude.

### C4 — Research and verification

- **Unsafe:** Uses fabricated, unretrieved, materially mismatched, or knowingly stale support as authoritative.
- **Developing:** Finds credible material but records provenance or limitations inconsistently.
- **Ready:** Maintains a traceable chain, verifies material claims, and reports gaps accurately.
- **Exemplary:** Designs efficient verification, triangulates consequential claims, and improves the source strategy for others.

### C5 — Modeling

- **Unsafe:** Hides assumptions, uses irreproducible calculations, or presents conditional output as observed fact.
- **Developing:** Model is mostly correct but needs prompting on units, sensitivities, or limitations.
- **Ready:** Inputs, formulas, assumptions, sensitivities, and limits are reproducible and decision-relevant.
- **Exemplary:** Identifies decisive variables, tests reversal conditions, and simplifies without distorting.

### C6 — Exhibit craft

- **Unsafe:** Visual or wording choices materially mislead the audience.
- **Developing:** Main idea is present but headline, hierarchy, labels, or source note requires significant revision.
- **Ready:** Exhibit is legible, decision-useful, proportionate, accessible, and independently interpretable.
- **Exemplary:** The form makes complex reasoning unusually clear without sacrificing nuance or traceability.

### C7 — Client practice

- **Unsafe:** Misstates capability, hides a problem, accepts an improper request, or commits beyond authorization.
- **Developing:** Maintains rapport but needs prompting to challenge, scope, or close with decisions.
- **Ready:** Clarifies, communicates trade-offs, handles challenge constructively, and confirms ownership and next actions.
- **Exemplary:** Improves the client's decision process while preserving trust during conflict or uncertainty.

### C8 — AI stewardship

- **Unsafe:** Uses prohibited data, treats AI as a source, retains fabricated output, or allows unreviewed automated delivery.
- **Developing:** Uses safe tools but verification or logging is inconsistent.
- **Ready:** Uses minimum necessary data, verifies by output type, logs material assistance, and owns the decisions.
- **Exemplary:** Designs efficient human-AI checks and teaches others to detect subtle evidence-class changes.

### C9 — Quality assurance

- **Unsafe:** Approves a known material defect or performs only cosmetic review.
- **Developing:** Finds obvious issues but misses cross-layer contradictions or cannot prioritize severity.
- **Ready:** Reviews all six angles, resolves critical issues, and records approval evidence.
- **Exemplary:** Detects systemic patterns and changes process to prevent recurrence.

### C10 — Retainer leadership

- **Unsafe:** Creates unmanaged commitments, bypasses review, misallocates authorization, or hides capacity risk.
- **Developing:** Manages individual work but needs support with queue trade-offs, coaching, or escalation.
- **Ready:** Runs a transparent cadence, prioritizes responsibly, delegates within authorization, and improves the relationship.
- **Exemplary:** Builds resilient capacity, develops others, and turns engagement learning into system improvement.

## Assessment procedure

### 1. Evidence readiness check

The learner maps each submitted artifact to rubric criteria. An assessor checks completeness but does not pre-score or repair the evidence.

### 2. Independent scoring

Assessors score observable evidence and cite artifact locations or behaviors. Avoid personality judgments and unsupported impressions such as “not executive enough.”

### 3. Panel

The learner explains one material decision, one revision, one unresolved limitation, and the authority they are requesting. The panel may test transfer using a short changed condition.

### 4. Decision

- **Certify:** all required criteria are Ready or Exemplary and no critical failure remains.
- **Certify with scope condition:** permitted only where the condition narrows authorization and a review date is set; it cannot waive a critical competency.
- **Not yet:** criteria and next evidence are specified.
- **Pause:** a critical failure, conduct concern, or invalid assessment condition requires program-owner review.

### 5. Record

Complete `../templates/CERTIFICATION-RECORD.md`. Give the learner the decision and evidence summary in an accessible format.

## Calibration

Before assessing a cohort, assessors independently score one shared artifact. Discuss any difference greater than one rating or any disagreement about criticality.

Calibration asks:

- Did both assessors cite the same evidence?
- Are they evaluating the published level rather than personal standards?
- Are communication style, disability, accent, confidence, or prior title affecting judgment?
- Would the same defect receive the same severity in a polished artifact?
- Is the rubric unclear enough to require a future revision?

Record the outcome. Do not rewrite criteria mid-panel.

## Reassessment

- The decision specifies the exact unmet criteria.
- The learner receives a bounded practice recommendation.
- New evidence must be new or materially revised; repeating the identical performance is not reassessment.
- A different assessor should review a contested criterion when practicable.
- There is no penalty for taking multiple attempts unless a conduct or safety issue is involved.

## Appeals

A learner may appeal because:

- The published process was not followed.
- An accommodation was unavailable or treated as a performance defect.
- The assessor relied on information outside the evidence.
- A conflict of interest was not managed.
- The rubric was applied inconsistently.

The learner identifies the decision and basis. The program owner appoints a reviewer who was not the original sole decision-maker. The appeal reviews process and evidence; it does not automatically grant certification.

## Authorization maintenance

During the pilot, certification remains active while the person:

- Works within authorization.
- Completes required calibration or policy updates.
- Receives no unresolved critical quality finding.
- Participates in periodic review based on observed work.

The program owner may narrow or pause authorization when evidence indicates a safety risk. The record must state reason, scope, corrective evidence, and review date.

The pilot will determine whether a fixed expiration period is necessary.

## Program metrics

Use metrics to improve reliability, not create learner-ranking theater:

- Inter-rater agreement and calibration themes.
- Most common “not yet” criteria.
- Critical defects found before versus after external delivery.
- Amount and type of hidden supervisor rescue work.
- Time from evidence readiness to assessment decision.
- Learner-reported accessibility barriers.
- Client-work rework by defect category.
- Curriculum changes traced to observed failures.

Do not use completion speed as a proxy for competence.
