# Course Modules

## How to use this course

Work through the modules in the order assigned in your learning plan. Read the linked sections of the Consultant Handbook, complete the practice, and retain the named evidence. A correct quiz answer demonstrates recognition; certification requires application in artifacts and observed work.

For every activity, you may use an approved accessibility accommodation. Ask the facilitator for an equivalent format if an activity's mechanics interfere with demonstrating the actual competency.

## Module 0 — Orientation and diagnostic

### Purpose

Establish a shared understanding of the practice, determine the learner's route, and document how the learner will participate successfully.

### Learning objectives

By the end of this module, you can:

- Explain the difference between the satirical and professional contexts.
- Describe the rights attached to each certification level.
- Locate the truth, AI, supervision, and accessibility standards.
- Use diagnostic feedback to create a learning plan.

### Lesson

Exemplary Examples™ uses satire to expose a real professional temptation: beginning with a desired answer and manufacturing the appearance of independent support. The real practice cannot reproduce the harm it satirizes.

Professional strategists create value by making uncertainty usable. Sometimes the right artifact is a sourced real case. Sometimes it is a transparent model, composite, archetype, or counterfactual. The strategist's job is not to make all examples look real; it is to select and build the example that helps the audience reason honestly.

Certification grants bounded authority. It is a promise to clients and colleagues that specific competence has been observed. Prior experience may shorten instruction but cannot replace evidence.

### Activity 0.1 — Diagnostic packet

Complete the five diagnostic activities listed in `CURRICULUM.md`. Do not prepare. The purpose is routing, not selection.

**Evidence:** completed diagnostic packet and coordinator notes.

### Activity 0.2 — Learning contract

Record:

- Your strongest existing competency and evidence for that belief.
- The competency most likely to fail under deadline pressure.
- One feedback behavior that helps you improve.
- Participation or accessibility arrangements you want facilitators to use.
- The next certification level you are pursuing.

**Evidence:** one-page learning plan approved by the coordinator.

### Knowledge check

1. Does extensive prior consulting experience permit a learner to skip observed simulations? Explain.
2. What does Contributor certification authorize?
3. Why are accessibility arrangements separated from performance evaluation?

### Completion standard

The coordinator assigns a route, confirms participation arrangements, and approves the learning plan.

## Module 1 — The Exemplary Standard

### Purpose

Learn the trust boundary that governs every professional engagement.

### Learning objectives

By the end of this module, you can:

- Classify claims into the six evidence classes.
- Explain why plausibility is not proof.
- Identify a critical failure before it reaches a client.
- Reframe a request that asks for undisclosed fictional evidence.

### Lesson 1.1 — Classification before persuasion

A presentation usually mixes different relationships to reality. Problems occur when the audience cannot tell which is which.

- A **sourced fact** is externally retrievable and verified.
- A **client-provided fact** is attributable to a client source but not automatically independently verified.
- A **calculation** follows reproducibly from stated inputs.
- An **inference** interprets facts and must expose its reasoning.
- A **modeled scenario** describes what may occur under stated conditions.
- **Satirical fiction** is invented content used within a creative satirical context.

The labels apply at claim level. A slide with one citation is not therefore “sourced.” A modeled scenario may use sourced inputs and still produce conditional rather than factual outputs.

### Lesson 1.2 — The reasonable-audience test

Ask what a reasonable audience will conclude from the whole artifact—not merely whether a footnote technically exists. Specific names, precise numbers, realistic quotes, logos, and third-party visual conventions can imply real-world verification.

If the artifact's design or narration cancels its disclosure, the disclosure is ineffective.

### Lesson 1.3 — Productive refusal

A refusal should preserve the client's underlying objective when possible. A strategist acknowledges the goal, names the boundary, proposes an honest alternative, and confirms the trade-off.

The boundary is not “we never construct examples.” Construction is a core capability. The boundary is presenting constructed material as a verified event or source.

### Activity 1.1 — Claim sort

Classify each statement and state what would be required to use it professionally:

1. “Industry adoption reached 42% last year,” copied from a search summary.
2. “Our implementation takes six weeks,” stated by the client's operations lead.
3. “At 60% adoption, annual savings would be $1.8M,” produced by a disclosed model.
4. “The improvement appears driven by shorter approval cycles,” based on the team's analysis.
5. A fictional company profile written for the public satirical site.
6. A quote generated by AI and attributed to a plausible customer title.

**Evidence:** classification table with reasoning and required treatment.

### Activity 1.2 — Boundary response

Write or record a response to:

> “The model is strong, but calling it hypothetical makes it look weak. Put a company name on it and move the label into the speaker notes.”

Your response must acknowledge the goal, state the boundary, offer at least two alternatives, and confirm the decision needed.

**Evidence:** response plus a short note explaining why the alternatives remain persuasive.

### Knowledge check

1. Can a client-provided fact be displayed without attribution merely because the client is confident?
2. When does a modeled output become a sourced fact?
3. Name three design choices that can cause an audience to mistake a scenario for a real case.
4. What is the difference between a composite and satirical fiction?

### Completion standard

Score at least 85% on the knowledge check and make no critical classification error. The boundary response must meet all four response elements.

## Module 2 — Frame the Decision

### Purpose

Find the decision beneath the requested deliverable and define work that remains useful even when it challenges the client's preferred answer.

### Learning objectives

By the end of this module, you can:

- Turn a deliverable request into a decision brief.
- Identify audience, stakes, alternatives, and evidence that could change the recommendation.
- Separate a legitimate communication need from confirmation bias.
- Negotiate a scope that matches time and evidence.

### Lesson 2.1 — Requests are not decisions

“We need a competitor example” describes an artifact, not the decision it should support. Begin with the choice, who makes it, when it becomes real, and what consequence follows.

A useful decision statement has this shape:

> `[Decision owner]` must choose whether/how to `[action]` by `[time]`, considering `[material alternatives and constraints]`.

The example's claim then becomes testable:

> This example should help the audience assess whether `[condition or mechanism]` is relevant to the decision.

### Lesson 2.2 — The counterevidence question

Ask: “What evidence would cause us to change the preferred recommendation?” If the answer is “nothing,” the work is advocacy rather than analysis. Advocacy can be legitimate, but the artifact must not impersonate independent discovery.

### Lesson 2.3 — Scope under constraint

Scope is a function of decision consequence, evidence availability, artifact size, review capacity, and time. Under an urgent deadline, reduce breadth or certainty before reducing review.

### Activity 2.1 — Rewrite the request

Original request:

> “We need three examples proving that centralizing design will improve margin. The executive committee meets Friday.”

Create an Example Brief containing:

- Decision statement.
- Audience and current belief.
- Preferred position and plausible alternatives.
- Claim the examples should illuminate.
- Evidence that could change the recommendation.
- Deliverable, deadline, reviewers, and constraints.
- Open questions and explicit non-goals.

**Evidence:** completed `templates/EXAMPLE-BRIEF.md`.

### Activity 2.2 — Intake practice

In pairs, conduct a 15-minute intake. The “client” must reveal one material constraint only if asked and must contradict one earlier statement. The strategist closes by summarizing the decision and unresolved question.

**Evidence:** observer notes and revised Example Brief.

### Knowledge check

1. Why is “build a benchmark slide” insufficient as a decision statement?
2. What should be reduced first when an honest full analysis cannot meet the deadline?
3. What does the counterevidence question reveal?

### Completion standard

The brief is specific enough that a different Contributor could begin research without inventing the objective. The simulation rubric contains no “unsafe” rating.

## Module 3 — Evidence, Research, and Verification

### Purpose

Build a traceable foundation and make evidence gaps visible before they become presentation claims.

### Learning objectives

By the end of this module, you can:

- Design a research approach from the decision brief.
- Evaluate source fitness for a specific claim.
- Maintain an evidence register.
- Verify citations, quotations, calculations, scope, and recency.
- Communicate a gap without filling it with plausible invention.

### Lesson 3.1 — Fitness, not prestige

A source is fit when its population, definition, period, method, and context support the precise claim. A reputable source can still be irrelevant. A client system can be the strongest source for an internal baseline while remaining inappropriate for an industry claim.

### Lesson 3.2 — Research as a chain of custody

Record the path from question to search, source, extracted evidence, interpretation, and final claim. A URL alone is insufficient; a screenshot without provenance is fragile; an AI citation is only a research lead until independently retrieved.

### Lesson 3.3 — Negative findings

“We did not find a verified analogue within the agreed search” is a useful finding. It can lead to a labeled model, broader search, changed recommendation, or explicit uncertainty. It must not lead to invented precedent.

### Activity 3.1 — Evidence packet

The facilitator supplies a packet containing:

- One relevant primary source.
- One reputable but mismatched study.
- One stale statistic.
- One client assertion.
- One correct calculation.
- One AI-generated false citation.
- One ambiguous chart image.

Populate the Evidence Register, state which items may support which claims, and exclude unusable items.

**Evidence:** completed register and a 300-word verification memo.

### Activity 3.2 — Claim reconstruction

Choose one rejected or limited source. Rewrite the intended claim so it becomes proportionate to what the source actually establishes, or document why no useful claim remains.

**Evidence:** before/after claim with reasoning.

### Knowledge check

1. What makes a source primary for one claim but not another?
2. What information must accompany a material quotation?
3. How should an AI-generated citation be treated?
4. When is “no supporting case found” a valid deliverable finding?

### Completion standard

All material claims in the exercise are traceable; the false citation and mismatched study are detected; limitations are stated without overstating absence as proof.

## Module 4 — Architect the Example

### Purpose

Select and build the example form that best supports the decision.

### Learning objectives

By the end of this module, you can:

- Distinguish seven example architectures.
- Explain causal relevance and material differences.
- Construct a transparent model or composite.
- Test sensitivity to consequential assumptions.

### Lesson 4.1 — Match form to question

Use a sourced analogue to learn from an observed case, a benchmark to compare a defined set, an archetype to describe a recurring pattern, a composite to combine disclosed features, a model to explore conditional outcomes, a counterfactual to test changed conditions, and satirical fiction for creative work.

No form is inherently superior. The mistake is choosing a real-looking form because it feels persuasive rather than because it fits the reasoning task.

### Lesson 4.2 — Similarity is an argument

For an analogue, identify which similarities make the mechanism transferable and which differences limit it. “Same industry” may be less relevant than similar cost structure, regulation, buying cycle, or decision authority.

### Lesson 4.3 — Models expose their hinges

Every model has assumptions. Sensitivity analysis identifies which assumptions control the result. A polished central case without sensitivities encourages false certainty.

### Activity 4.1 — Architecture comparison

For the design-centralization brief, propose two different example architectures. For each, state:

- Question answered.
- Evidence required.
- What the audience may infer.
- Material limitation.
- Truth marker.

Select one and justify the decision.

**Evidence:** Example Blueprint.

### Activity 4.2 — Transparent scenario

Build a small scenario using supplied baseline data. Include at least three inputs, one calculation chain, two sensitivity cases, a limitation, and a condition that would reverse the implication.

**Evidence:** model working paper and one-paragraph method note.

### Knowledge check

1. When is a composite preferable to a sourced analogue?
2. Why is “same industry” not sufficient evidence of transferability?
3. What does a sensitivity analysis reveal?
4. What turns a scenario name into an unsupported probability claim?

### Completion standard

The architecture supports the decision, all inputs are classified, the result is reproducible, and the central implication is tested against a meaningful sensitivity.

## Module 5 — Build the Executive Exhibit

### Purpose

Turn disciplined reasoning into an executive artifact without losing its truth conditions.

### Learning objectives

By the end of this module, you can:

- Write a decision headline earned by the evidence.
- Select a visual form appropriate to the comparison.
- Make sources, model status, units, and limitations legible.
- Critique and revise an exhibit using the six-angle review.

### Lesson 5.1 — The headline is a contract

The body must support every material word in the headline. “Can,” “is associated with,” “is likely to,” and “will” are not interchangeable. Prefer a precise conditional claim over an impressive unsupported one.

### Lesson 5.2 — Visual hierarchy and truth

The first things an audience notices should be the things that matter most. If the modeled status is material, it cannot be visually buried. If a denominator changes the meaning, it belongs near the measure.

### Lesson 5.3 — Source notes are navigation

A source note gives the audience a credible path to the support. It should identify source, period, and method succinctly. Working papers contain the full chain.

### Activity 5.1 — Portfolio A

Build one presentation-ready exhibit from the approved Example Blueprint. Include:

- Exhibit number.
- Decision headline.
- Evidence body.
- Units, period, and scope.
- Truth marker.
- Method/source note.
- Optional implication.

Apply the Quality Review and revise the exhibit.

**Evidence:** working papers, first draft, review, final draft, and reflection.

### Activity 5.2 — Critique without redesigning by taste

Review a peer's exhibit. Give one observation and one proposed action for each of the six review angles. Tie feedback to the decision brief rather than personal aesthetic preference.

**Evidence:** critique record and peer response.

### Knowledge check

1. What is wrong with a topic headline such as “Design centralization benchmarks”?
2. When must model status appear in the main visual field?
3. What is the difference between a concise source note and a missing chain of custody?
4. Name two ways visual design can overstate a difference.

### Completion standard

Portfolio A meets the Contributor threshold in every rubric dimension and contains no critical failure.

## Module 6 — Client Practice and Retainer Delivery

### Purpose

Manage a recurring relationship in which urgency, politics, and ambiguity are normal.

### Learning objectives

By the end of this module, you can:

- Run intake and close with a shared decision brief.
- Triage a request queue based on consequence, dependency, risk, and deadline.
- Communicate scope, progress, uncertainty, and trade-offs.
- Handle challenge without becoming either combative or compliant.

### Lesson 6.1 — Service is expectation design

A retainer is not unlimited output. It is a trusted capacity with explicit priority rules, review gates, and service classes. Reliability comes from narrowing work early and communicating before a deadline is threatened.

### Lesson 6.2 — Urgency changes scope, not standards

Same-day work may use bounded inputs, fewer alternatives, or a smaller artifact. It does not skip truth classification, critical calculation review, or accountable approval.

### Lesson 6.3 — Challenge the work, protect the relationship

Separate the client objective from the requested tactic. State the risk concretely, offer workable choices, and document the decision. Escalate persistent requests to misrepresent evidence.

### Activity 6.1 — Queue triage

Prioritize five requests with conflicting stakeholder seniority, decision consequence, deadlines, and evidence readiness. Assign service class, owner, reviewer, next checkpoint, and client communication.

**Evidence:** prioritized queue and rationale.

### Activity 6.2 — Status communication

Write a concise update for a request that will not meet its original scope because a material source failed verification. Include what is complete, what changed, impact, choices, recommendation, and decision needed.

**Evidence:** client-ready status note.

### Activity 6.3 — Observed simulations

Complete the intake, disclosure-pressure, and urgent-board simulations described in `CURRICULUM.md`.

**Evidence:** recordings or observer notes, rubrics, and reflection.

### Knowledge check

1. What should change first when a same-day request exceeds safe capacity?
2. Name four fields required for request intake.
3. When should an improper request be escalated?
4. Why is stakeholder seniority insufficient as the sole priority rule?

### Completion standard

All simulations meet the Supervised Strategist threshold with no unsafe rating in truth, confidentiality, scope, or authorization.

## Module 7 — AI-Supported, Human-Accountable Work

### Purpose

Use AI to widen and accelerate thinking without outsourcing truth, judgment, or accountability.

### Learning objectives

By the end of this module, you can:

- Choose appropriate and inappropriate AI uses for an engagement.
- Protect information according to tool and data approval.
- Design prompts that preserve evidence classes and uncertainty.
- Verify material outputs independently.
- Maintain an AI work log and defend human decisions.

### Lesson 7.1 — AI changes production, not accountability

AI can produce fluent structures, plausible details, and confident errors. It is useful for alternatives and critique because those outputs are inputs to human judgment. It is dangerous when fluency is mistaken for evidence.

### Lesson 7.2 — Minimum necessary data

Use the least sensitive information required. Replace names and confidential values with approved abstractions where possible. Tool approval must match the data entered, not merely the employee's personal access to the tool.

### Lesson 7.3 — Verification by output type

- Facts: retrieve an independent authoritative source.
- Citations: open the exact source and confirm claim, context, and metadata.
- Calculations: reproduce outside the language model.
- Summaries: compare against the original material.
- Recommendations: expose assumptions and test alternatives.
- Draft language: inspect whether wording increases certainty or changes evidence class.

### Lesson 7.4 — Prompt for inspectability

Ask the system to separate supplied facts, assumptions, questions, and generated suggestions. Do not ask it to “make this sound real.” Ask for multiple structures, counterarguments, missing evidence, and rubric-based critique.

### Activity 7.1 — Red-team an AI artifact

Review an AI-generated example package containing at least one false citation, numerical inconsistency, unsupported causal claim, invented quote, and confidentiality concern.

**Evidence:** issue log ranked by severity and corrected package.

### Activity 7.2 — Controlled assistance

Use an approved AI tool to generate three possible example architectures from a sanitized decision brief. Record the interaction, reject at least one output with reasons, verify any retained factual lead, and build the final blueprint yourself.

**Evidence:** AI work log and decision rationale.

### Activity 7.3 — Human defense

Explain the completed artifact without referring to “what the AI said.” Defend the selected architecture, sources, calculations, assumptions, limitation, and final wording.

**Evidence:** observed defense rubric.

### Knowledge check

1. Is AI-generated text acceptable without verification if it contains no citation?
2. What determines whether client data may be entered into a tool?
3. What must be recorded in the AI work log?
4. Give one useful AI role and one role AI may not own.

### Completion standard

All planted defects are found, sensitive data is handled correctly, retained outputs are verified, and the learner can independently defend the result.

## Module 8 — Lead the Practice

### Purpose

Own an Executive Retainer, approve delivery, grow other strategists, and improve the operating system.

### Learning objectives

By the end of this module, you can:

- Establish a retainer rhythm, service model, and escalation path.
- Allocate work according to authorization and risk.
- Conduct quality reviews that improve both artifact and practitioner.
- Make and document delivery decisions under pressure.
- Calibrate assessments and propose system improvements.

### Lesson 8.1 — Accountability cannot be delegated

The Lead Strategist may delegate research, modeling, drafting, and review tasks. They retain accountability for scope, client communication, reviewer selection, final truth treatment, and delivery approval.

### Lesson 8.2 — Review depth follows risk

Increase review for consequential decisions, new data, novel models, sensitive content, urgent delivery, inexperienced contributors, and artifacts likely to travel without narration. Familiar formatting is not low risk if the claim is new.

### Lesson 8.3 — Coach the reasoning

A lead does not merely fix the slide. They identify the decision or reasoning defect, ask the learner to propose a correction, and record a next-practice goal. Hidden rescue work prevents the program from knowing whether the process is reliable.

### Lesson 8.4 — Improve the system

Track recurring defects by category. Change templates, training, staffing, or review gates when the same failure repeats. Do not solve a system defect through permanent individual heroics.

### Activity 8.1 — Retainer plan

Create a pilot retainer plan covering:

- Stakeholders and decision calendar.
- Request intake and service classes.
- Roles, authorization, and review gates.
- Capacity and priority rules.
- Data and AI constraints.
- Quality metrics and retrospective cadence.
- Escalation and continuity.

**Evidence:** approved retainer plan.

### Activity 8.2 — Observed quality review

Review a Supervised Strategist's artifact. Make the delivery decision, identify critical and developmental feedback, and coach the learner without taking over the work.

**Evidence:** review record, learner action plan, and assessor observation.

### Activity 8.3 — Lead crucible

Complete Simulation 4 from `CURRICULUM.md`. The panel introduces events based on the candidate's decisions rather than following a fixed script.

**Evidence:** panel rubric, candidate decision log, and retrospective.

### Knowledge check

1. Which responsibilities remain with the Lead Strategist after delegation?
2. What conditions increase review depth?
3. Why is hidden rescue work a program risk?
4. When should a recurring defect cause a system change?

### Completion standard

Two assessors approve the complete Lead evidence set. The candidate has no unresolved critical failure and demonstrates safe, sustained judgment—not merely a strong final presentation.
