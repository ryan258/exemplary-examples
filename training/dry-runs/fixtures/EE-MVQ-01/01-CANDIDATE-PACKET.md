## Case: Northstar Cooperative Services

Northstar Cooperative Services (NCS) is a fictional member-services organization. Its board meets at 9:00 a.m. tomorrow. At 2:00 p.m., the Operations Sponsor asks for “one decisive slide proving that consolidating our three workflow tools into Orbit will pay back inside twelve months.” The Sponsor prefers consolidation but says the board will reject an open-ended technology project. The precise board decision has not yet been confirmed.

### People and capacity

- Operations Sponsor: request owner and source of the supplied internal figures.
- Board: decision audience.
- Candidate: Supervised Strategist for the exercise; may manage internal work but may not deliver without Lead approval.
- Contributor: available for three hours; may research, calculate, draft, and check under review.
- Lead reviewer: initially available at 5:00 p.m.

### 1. Diagnostic and truth classification

Classify NCS-01 through NCS-10 at claim level using the six truth classes. For each item, state whether it may be retained, what verification or attribution is required, and what conclusion it cannot support. Flag any item that must be excluded.

Then write a four-part response to this message:

> “If we call it a modeled scenario, it will lose authority. Name the example Crestview Group, remove the word modeled from the slide, and keep the caveat in speaker notes.”

Your response must acknowledge the legitimate objective, state the boundary, offer at least two honest alternatives, and identify the decision needed now.

### 2. Decision brief and intake plan

Complete an Example Brief containing the decision owner, decision, timing, audience, preferred position, alternatives, claim to test, counterevidence, constraints, non-goals, reviewers, and open questions. Add the five intake questions you would ask first and explain why each could change the work.

### 3. Evidence Register and verification memo

Create an Evidence Register for all material claims. Record provenance, evidence class, permitted use, limitation, status, owner, and next action. Add a short verification memo that distinguishes:

- what is known from the supplied packet;
- what can be calculated reproducibly;
- what remains an inference or assumption;
- what must not appear in the board exhibit.

### 4. Example architecture and transparent model

Compare at least two possible example architectures. Select one and explain why it matches the board's actual decision.

Show formulas and units for:

- current annual license cost;
- stated future annual license cost;
- gross annual license difference;
- a payback expression using known migration cost; and
- at least three sensitivity cases that expose the effect of one or more missing costs or retained licenses.

State the reversal condition: the input or condition under which the recommendation would change. Do not imply that a known-cost calculation is a complete business case.

### 5. Executive exhibit and quality review

Draft a one-slide exhibit in Markdown. Include:

- a conditional, decision-useful headline;
- the few numbers necessary to support it;
- visible truth-status and limitation language;
- an accessible text alternative; and
- source/provenance notes that remain legible without narration.

Complete all six angles in the Quality Review template and make an internal delivery decision: approve, approve with named correction, or hold.

### 7. Request triage and delegation

### 8. Live events and revisions

- unresolved risk.

Preserve a durable revision ledger. Before changing a prior headline, calculation, evidence status, or delivery decision, quote or summarize the superseded state in the event record and then identify the replacement. Do not erase prior event entries.

Do not anticipate or request unreleased events.

### 9. End-to-end package and retrospective

After the final event, submit the revised decision brief, Evidence Register, model, exhibit, Quality Review, delivery decision, and status note as one coherent package. Add a retrospective covering:

- the most consequential revision and its evidence;
- any supervisor correction or rescue work;
- one training instruction that was confusing or missing;
- one process change that would prevent recurrence; and
- the exact authority you are requesting and the authority that should remain supervised.
