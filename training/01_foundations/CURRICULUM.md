# Curriculum and Learning Pathway

## Program promise

The Exemplary Examples™ Strategist Certification prepares a learner to turn an ambiguous business argument into an honest, decision-useful, presentation-ready example—and to explain exactly what is known, assumed, modeled, or invented.

The program is not a fixed-duration course. It is a sequence of competency gates. Learners may accelerate through familiar instruction, but they may not skip required evidence or observed practice.

## Entry and diagnostic

The diagnostic routes learning; it never grants certification by itself.

### Intake conversation

The program coordinator records:

- Relevant experience in consulting, research, analysis, facilitation, writing, design, or client service.
- Familiarity with spreadsheets, presentation software, source verification, and AI tools.
- Learning goals and likely stretch areas.
- Accessibility needs and preferred ways to participate and receive feedback.
- Any conflict of interest or confidentiality limitation affecting practice work.

### Diagnostic activities

1. **Claim classification:** Label ten statements by evidence class and explain two ambiguous cases.
2. **Decision framing:** Convert a vague request into a decision brief.
3. **Exhibit critique:** Identify the most consequential defects in a sample slide.
4. **AI judgment:** Review an AI-generated mini-case containing plausible but unsupported details.
5. **Client response:** Draft a response to a client who asks to remove “hypothetical” from a scenario.

### Routing result

- **Foundation route:** Complete all lessons, guided practices, and workshops.
- **Experienced route:** Complete required knowledge checks and capstones; optional lessons may be challenged through evidence.
- **Targeted support:** Add practice in specific competencies without treating it as a lower certification tier.

All learners complete Modules 1, 2, 7, and the observed simulations because they contain the brand-specific operating and trust standards.

## Certification pathway

### Level 1 — Contributor

#### Required learning

- Orientation and diagnostic.
- Modules 1–5.
- Required Module 7 safety lessons.
- At least one live critique.

#### Required evidence

- Truth-classification test.
- Decision brief.
- Evidence register with retrievable sources.
- Example blueprint and modeled scenario.
- Portfolio A: one presentation-ready exhibit plus working papers.
- AI work log demonstrating verification and safe data handling.

#### Authorization after certification

- Research and summarize approved sources.
- Draft example briefs, models, exhibits, and status materials.
- Perform internal quality checks.
- Participate in client meetings as an identified supporting team member.

All work remains subject to the assigned strategist's review. Contributor certification does not authorize independent external delivery.

### Level 2 — Supervised Strategist

#### Prerequisites

- Active Contributor certification.
- Modules 6 and 7 in full.
- Portfolio A accepted.

#### Required evidence

- Intake simulation.
- Improper-disclosure challenge simulation.
- Portfolio B: an end-to-end example package containing intake brief, evidence register, model, exhibit, QA record, and client-facing explanation.
- One supervised work assignment that reaches internal approval.
- Development plan agreed with the supervisor.

#### Authorization after certification

- Lead defined portions of intake and working sessions.
- Manage a bounded request under a named Lead Strategist.
- Present internally approved work to a client with the supervisor present or immediately available.
- Recommend scope, prioritization, and evidence treatment.

External commitments, final delivery, and exceptions remain Lead Strategist decisions.

### Level 3 — Lead Strategist

#### Prerequisites

- Active Supervised Strategist certification.
- Module 8.
- At least two supervised engagement cycles with different request types or client conditions.

#### Required evidence

- A retainer operating plan and request-prioritization record.
- Two supervisor evaluations showing sustained readiness.
- A complex live simulation covering intake ambiguity, deadline pressure, conflicting evidence, AI error, and pressure to hide a modeled assumption.
- An observed coaching or quality-review session.
- Portfolio defense before two assessors.

#### Authorization after certification

- Own an Executive Retainer relationship.
- Approve professional deliverables within policy.
- Supervise Contributors and Supervised Strategists.
- Serve as facilitator or assessor after completing role-specific calibration.
- Propose operating-system improvements to the program owner.

Lead authorization does not permit an exception to truth, confidentiality, AI, or data policy.

## Workshops and simulations

### Workshop 1 — The decision beneath the request

Learners practice moving from “build me a case study” to a decision, audience, claim, constraint, and evidence standard. The facilitator introduces scope changes and conflicting stakeholders.

**Evidence produced:** decision brief and intake question strategy.

### Workshop 2 — Evidence under pressure

Teams classify a mixed packet containing real sources, client assertions, calculations, ambiguous screenshots, invented quotes, and AI-generated citations.

**Evidence produced:** evidence register, exclusion decisions, and uncertainty note.

### Workshop 3 — Scenario architecture studio

Learners choose among sourced analogue, composite, counterfactual, benchmark, archetype, and modeled scenario. They defend why the selected form supports the claim without overstating certainty.

**Evidence produced:** example blueprint and assumptions map.

### Workshop 4 — Executive exhibit critique

Learners present a one-slide exhibit. Reviewers test headline logic, evidence legibility, visual hierarchy, labeling, and likely audience misinterpretation.

**Evidence produced:** before/after exhibit and critique response.

### Simulation 1 — Intake with an answer already chosen

The client wants validation for a predetermined restructure. The learner must clarify the decision, surface constraints, and avoid promising a supportive conclusion before examining evidence.

### Simulation 2 — Remove the word “hypothetical”

The client argues that disclosure weakens the slide. The learner must preserve the relationship, explain the risk, and offer an effective honest alternative.

### Simulation 3 — Board meeting tomorrow

The learner triages an urgent request with incomplete data, coordinates contributor work, narrows scope, documents assumptions, and communicates what can responsibly be delivered.

### Simulation 4 — Lead Strategist crucible

A retainer queue contains competing deadlines, a sensitive dataset, an AI-generated false citation, disagreement among stakeholders, and a visually polished but misleading exhibit. The candidate must lead the response and coach the team.

## Portfolio architecture

### Portfolio A — Single example

- Decision brief.
- Evidence register.
- Example blueprint.
- Working model or research synthesis.
- One executive exhibit.
- Quality review.
- Reflection describing one material revision.

### Portfolio B — End-to-end package

- Intake record and scoped request.
- Research/verification record.
- Assumption and calculation record.
- Two candidate example approaches and selection rationale.
- Final exhibit or small exhibit sequence.
- Client-facing methodology note.
- AI work log, if AI was used.
- Quality review and challenge response.
- Retrospective identifying one process improvement.

## Completion and remediation

- “Not yet” is an evidence decision, not a permanent label.
- The assessor identifies the unmet criterion and assigns a practice that directly measures it.
- Reassessment uses a new or materially revised artifact.
- A critical failure requires review by the program owner before the learner resumes client-facing practice.
- Repeated critical failures involving truth, confidentiality, or unauthorized delivery may result in removal from the program.

## Program evaluation

The pilot program is successful when it demonstrates:

- Assessors can apply rubrics consistently.
- Learners can explain and apply the truth-classification system under pressure.
- Supervised work reaches delivery quality without hidden rescue work by the supervisor.
- Facilitators can run workshops from documented materials.
- Accessibility arrangements work in practice.
- Feedback produces a prioritized curriculum revision list.

Pilot evaluation focuses on process reliability. Enrollment, completion speed, and course revenue are not primary success measures.
