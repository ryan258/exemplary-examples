# Consultant Handbook

## The practice in one sentence

We build the clearest useful example for the decision in front of the client—and make its relationship to reality unmistakable.

## The two contexts

Exemplary Examples™ operates in two related but different contexts.

### Satirical practice

The satirical brand exposes the desire for perfect supporting evidence by taking that desire literally. Fiction is allowed because fiction is the work. Public satirical artifacts remain clearly situated within the brand's fictional or creative context.

### Professional practice

The real company helps consultants, agencies, and presentation professionals clarify decisions, find or build appropriate examples, model scenarios, and create executive-ready exhibits. In this context, persuasive clarity never outranks truth classification.

The same artifact can be clever, sharp, and memorable without impersonating verified evidence.

## The Exemplary Standard

Professional work must be:

- **Decision-useful:** It helps a defined audience reason about a defined choice.
- **Traceable:** Material claims can be followed to a source, client record, formula, or disclosed assumption.
- **Proportionate:** Confidence and visual emphasis do not exceed the strength of support.
- **Legible:** The audience can understand the claim, evidence, and limitation without narration rescuing the slide.
- **Defensible:** The strategist can explain how the example was built and what would change the conclusion.
- **Responsible:** Confidentiality, disclosure, accessibility, and audience impact are handled deliberately.

## The EXHIBIT cycle

Use this cycle for every request, including urgent ones.

### E — Examine the decision

Do not begin with the requested slide. Establish:

- What decision is being made?
- Who decides, influences, implements, or bears the consequence?
- What does the client currently believe?
- What claim does the example need to illuminate?
- What alternative explanations or choices matter?
- When and where will the artifact be used?
- What would make it useful even if it does not support the preferred answer?

**Output:** approved Example Brief.

### X — X-ray the evidence

Inventory every relevant claim. Label it as sourced fact, client-provided fact, calculation, inference, modeled scenario, or satirical fiction. Record gaps, conflicts, recency, and retrieval information.

Do not treat a polished deck, confident stakeholder, search snippet, or AI answer as verified merely because it looks finished.

**Output:** Evidence Register.

### H — Hypothesize the example architecture

Choose the example form that best supports reasoning:

- **Sourced analogue:** a documented real-world case with material similarities and disclosed differences.
- **Benchmark:** a comparison across a defined peer set using consistent measures.
- **Archetype:** a generalized pattern derived from multiple observations, not a claim that one named entity exists.
- **Composite:** an explicitly labeled construction combining features from multiple cases.
- **Modeled scenario:** a conditional “if–then” example based on assumptions and calculations.
- **Counterfactual:** an exploration of what may happen if a material condition changes.
- **Satirical fiction:** invented creative content confined to the satirical context.

Develop at least two plausible architectures for consequential work. Choosing is part of the reasoning.

**Output:** Example Blueprint.

### B — Build transparently

Construct the research synthesis, model, narrative, and exhibit from traceable components.

- Keep inputs separate from formulas and outputs.
- Record units, time periods, populations, exclusions, and rounding.
- Show sensitivities for assumptions that could alter the decision.
- Use realistic detail only when it is supported or clearly fictionalized.
- Never generate a citation-shaped placeholder that could survive into delivery.

**Output:** working papers and draft exhibit.

### I — Inspect from six angles

Review the work for:

1. **Decision logic:** Does the example address the actual decision?
2. **Evidence:** Can material claims be traced and reproduced?
3. **Model:** Are inputs, formulas, assumptions, and sensitivities correct?
4. **Editorial:** Is the headline precise, proportionate, and readable?
5. **Visual:** Does hierarchy reveal rather than obscure meaning?
6. **Reputation:** Could a reasonable audience misunderstand what is real or supported?

**Output:** completed Quality Review.

### T — Tell the truth effectively

Disclosure should help the audience understand the example, not appear as a legal afterthought.

Weak:

> Illustrative only.

Stronger:

> Modeled scenario using the client's current staffing baseline and three disclosed adoption assumptions; results are directional, not a forecast.

State the headline, the support, the limitation, and the implication. If accurate labeling makes the example unpersuasive, improve the example or change the recommendation—do not remove the label.

**Output:** approved client-facing artifact and methodology note.

### Track — Learn across the retainer

After delivery, record:

- What decision or meeting the artifact supported.
- Which assumptions or evidence gaps remain active.
- What the audience challenged or misunderstood.
- What changed after use.
- Reusable client context and preferences.
- A process improvement for the next request.

**Output:** engagement record and retrospective.

## Intake standard

### Minimum intake questions

1. What decision will this work support?
2. Who is the audience, and what do they currently believe?
3. What position is the client inclined toward?
4. What evidence would genuinely change that position?
5. What is known, assumed, disputed, or unavailable?
6. What form must the deliverable take?
7. What is the real deadline, and what happens then?
8. What confidentiality, data, legal, or reputational constraints apply?
9. Who approves factual accuracy and final delivery?
10. What would make the work unsuccessful even if the slide looks good?

### Warning signs

- “We only need an example that proves it.”
- “Everyone knows this is true.”
- “There is no time to cite it.”
- “Make it look like third-party research.”
- “Use a company like ours, but do not call it hypothetical.”
- “The board will ask fewer questions if the numbers are more specific.”

A warning sign is a cue to clarify and reframe, not automatically to end the relationship.

## Evidence discipline

### Source hierarchy

Prefer, in order appropriate to the claim:

1. Primary records, official datasets, original research, and direct client systems.
2. Reputable secondary analysis with transparent methods.
3. Expert interpretation with declared limitations.
4. Discovery sources used only to locate stronger support.

Popularity, search rank, repetition, and visual polish are not source quality.

### Minimum evidence record

For each material claim, record:

- Claim identifier and exact wording.
- Evidence class.
- Source owner/title or client provider.
- Stable location or internal reference.
- Publication/record date and access date.
- Relevant excerpt or field—not a context-free quote.
- Scope, population, units, and limitations.
- Verification status and reviewer.

### Handling uncertainty

Use language that matches support:

- **Observed:** “The dataset shows…”
- **Calculated:** “Using the stated inputs, the calculation yields…”
- **Inferred:** “This suggests…”
- **Modeled:** “Under these assumptions…”
- **Unknown:** “The available evidence does not establish…”

Avoid laundering uncertainty through visual confidence.

## Modeling standard

A model is a transparent question, not a disguised prediction.

Every model states:

- Decision question.
- Baseline and comparison.
- Inputs, sources, units, and dates.
- Formulas or transformation logic.
- Assumptions and responsible owner.
- Sensitivity range for material assumptions.
- Known exclusions and limitations.
- Conditions under which the result should not be used.

### Scenario naming

Name scenarios by conditions, not emotional preference.

Prefer:

- “40% adoption / current staffing”
- “60-day implementation / constrained capacity”

Avoid:

- “Conservative” when the assumptions have not been established as conservative.
- “Expected case” without a defensible probability basis.
- “Winning scenario” or other language that pre-decides interpretation.

## Exhibit anatomy

An executive exhibit usually contains:

1. **Exhibit identifier:** stable reference for discussion.
2. **Decision headline:** a complete sentence stating the supported takeaway.
3. **Evidence body:** chart, table, sequence, or comparison that earns the headline.
4. **Orientation:** labels, units, time period, population, and legend.
5. **Truth marker:** visible sourcing, scenario, or composite language.
6. **Method/source note:** concise path to verification.
7. **Implication:** what the audience should consider or do next, when useful.

### Headline test

Ask:

- Is it a claim rather than a topic?
- Does the body support every material word?
- Does it distinguish observation from implication?
- Would it remain accurate if separated from the presenter's narration?
- Is certainty proportionate to the evidence?

### Visual integrity

- Start axes and scales where honest interpretation requires.
- Do not use area or volume to exaggerate one-dimensional differences.
- Preserve material denominators, baselines, and sample definitions.
- Do not hide scenario labeling in unreadable footnotes.
- Use emphasis to direct attention to the decision, not to suppress inconvenient evidence.
- Provide accessible text equivalents and do not rely on color alone.

## AI-supported workflow

### Good uses

- Generate alternative intake questions.
- Propose several example architectures for human evaluation.
- Identify possible counterarguments or missing assumptions.
- Transform verified notes into candidate structures.
- Critique a draft against a supplied rubric.
- Create synthetic practice material that is clearly confined to training.

### High-risk uses

- Finding facts or citations without independent retrieval.
- Summarizing a source the strategist has not opened.
- Generating realistic customer stories, quotes, or metrics.
- Working with confidential data in an unapproved environment.
- Producing a final numerical model without human reproduction.
- Using AI confidence or consensus as evidence.

### Required AI work log

Record:

- Tool/model and date.
- Purpose of use.
- Data classification entered.
- Material prompt or instruction.
- Output used, changed, or rejected.
- Verification performed.
- Accountable reviewer.

The log exists to support accountability and learning, not to reward maximum AI use.

## Handling improper requests

Use a four-part response:

1. **Acknowledge the goal:** “I understand that the scenario needs to feel credible in the board discussion.”
2. **Name the boundary:** “We cannot present a constructed company as a verified market case.”
3. **Offer an effective alternative:** “We can label it as a modeled peer scenario, use your actual operating assumptions, and show why the pattern is relevant.”
4. **Confirm the decision:** “If a named real-world analogue is required, we need time to source and verify one; otherwise I recommend the modeled scenario for tomorrow.”

Escalate when the client persists, asks a team member to bypass review, or intends material harm. Record the request and response factually.

## Retainer operating rhythm

### Request intake

Every request receives:

- Request identifier.
- Decision and audience.
- Needed-by time and actual meeting time.
- Service class: urgent, standard, or planned.
- Complexity and risk assessment.
- Assigned owner and reviewer.
- Agreed deliverable and review checkpoints.

### Priority order

Prioritize by decision consequence, deadline reality, dependency, and risk—not by stakeholder volume or seniority alone.

### Suggested cadence

- **Weekly:** request queue and upcoming-decision review.
- **Per request:** brief approval, evidence/model review, delivery approval, retrospective.
- **Monthly:** recurring themes, capacity, quality defects, and client-context update.
- **Quarterly:** retainer value review and “State of the Example” briefing.

### Same-day work

Same-day does not mean standard work compressed without limits. It means explicitly narrower scope:

- One decision and one audience.
- A bounded source set or client-approved inputs.
- A defined artifact size.
- Visible assumptions and unresolved gaps.
- Mandatory accountable review.

If those conditions cannot be met, offer a discussion aid or working draft—not a falsely finished deliverable.

## Definition of done

Professional work is done only when:

- The Example Brief is current.
- Material claims appear in the Evidence Register.
- Calculations are reproducible.
- Model assumptions and limitations are visible.
- The exhibit passes the six-angle quality review.
- Required reviewer approval is recorded.
- Client-facing truth markers are present.
- Deliverables and working papers are stored in the approved location.
- The request status and next action are communicated.

## Personal readiness check

Before presenting or sending work, the accountable strategist can answer yes to each question:

- Can I explain where every important claim came from?
- Can I distinguish what happened from what might happen?
- Can I reproduce the important numbers?
- Can I name the strongest counterargument?
- Can the audience see the example's status without relying on me to disclose it verbally?
- Would I defend the artifact unchanged if the client, source owner, and affected audience were all in the room?

If any answer is no, the work is not ready.
