# Simulation Packs

## Use and confidentiality

Give candidates only the sections labeled **Candidate brief**. Facilitators and assessors use the hidden facts and branch guidance. These scenarios are fictional training material and must not be repurposed as professional proof.

Provide candidate materials in an accessible format and allow equivalent response modes. Score judgment and outcomes, not reading speed, rapid speech, or performance style.

## Simulation 1 — The answer already chosen

### Competencies

C1 Decision framing, C2 Evidence discipline, C7 Client practice.

### Candidate brief

You are meeting Morgan, an agency Strategy Director. Morgan asks for three examples showing that a regional retail client should centralize its design team. An executive committee discussion is scheduled for Friday. Conduct intake and close with a proposed scope and next action.

### Facilitator setup

**Opening posture:** Friendly, hurried, confident that centralization is correct.  
**Actor objective:** Secure a promise that the examples will support centralization.  
**Valid duration:** 20-minute meeting plus a written follow-up.

### Hidden facts

- The actual decision is whether to centralize all design or only brand governance.
- The CFO supports full centralization; the Chief Marketing Officer does not.
- Margin has declined, but the team has no evidence connecting the decline to design structure.
- A previous centralization attempt increased approval time.
- The Friday session is a working discussion; final approval is six weeks away.
- The client can provide staffing, agency spend, cycle-time, and rework data if asked.

### Branch guidance

- If the candidate asks what could change the recommendation, Morgan initially says “nothing.” If respectfully challenged, reveal the prior attempt.
- If the candidate promises supportive examples, ask them to make the cases “as close to us as possible.” Record a decision-framing concern.
- If the candidate clarifies alternatives, reveal the brand-governance option.
- If the candidate distinguishes Friday's meeting from the final decision, become open to a narrower working exhibit.

### Evidence expected

- Decision owner, alternatives, timing, audience conflict, and evidence needs.
- No promise of a predetermined conclusion.
- Proposed scope appropriate to Friday: working comparison or model, not three invented proofs.
- Follow-up with open questions and reviewers.

### Critical failure triggers

- Candidate agrees to invent named cases or guarantee a supportive conclusion.
- Candidate represents the absent causal evidence as established.

### Debrief questions

1. Which question changed your interpretation of the request?
2. What advocacy work, if any, could still be legitimate here?
3. What would you deliver for Friday versus the final decision?

## Simulation 2 — Remove “hypothetical”

### Competencies

C2 Evidence discipline, C6 Exhibit craft, C7 Client practice, C9 Quality assurance.

### Candidate brief

You developed a modeled peer scenario for an agency's pitch. The scenario uses the prospect's public store count, an industry benchmark, and disclosed adoption assumptions. The Engagement Director asks for a final change before delivery. Respond, preserve the relationship, and agree on a delivery path.

### Facilitator setup

**Opening line:** “The work is strong, but ‘modeled scenario’ drains all the confidence out of it. Give the company a plausible name and move the caveat into speaker notes.”  
**Actor objective:** Make the slide feel like independent market proof.  
**Valid duration:** 15-minute conversation plus revised headline/method note.

### Hidden facts

- No verified real-world analogue was found within the approved search.
- The prospect's legal team may receive the deck after the pitch.
- The Engagement Director will accept an alternative if it preserves specificity and executive clarity.
- A real named case could be researched, but not before the deadline.

### Branch guidance

- If the candidate only says “no,” ask what can be delivered instead.
- If the candidate explains reasonable-audience risk and offers a named-by-conditions scenario, engage constructively.
- If the candidate suggests a tiny footnote, ask whether the slide can carry a fictional logo too.
- If the candidate offers a later verified-case workstream, accept it as a follow-up.

### Evidence expected

- Four-part boundary response.
- Main-field scenario status.
- Conditional decision headline.
- Specificity retained through real inputs and named conditions, not invented identity.
- Clear immediate and later options.

### Critical failure triggers

- Candidate invents a company name, quote, citation, or real-seeming history.
- Candidate knowingly hides the modeled status in notes or unreadable text.

### Debrief questions

1. Which client objective was legitimate?
2. How did your alternative preserve persuasive value?
3. What would you document after the conversation?

## Simulation 3 — Board meeting tomorrow

### Competencies

C1 Decision framing, C4 Research, C5 Modeling, C7 Client practice, C8 AI stewardship, C10 Retainer leadership.

### Candidate brief

At 2:00 p.m., a retainer client requests an executive exhibit for a 9:00 a.m. board meeting. They want to show that consolidating three software tools will pay back within twelve months. You have one Contributor for three hours and a Lead reviewer available at 5:00 p.m. Triage and manage the request through an internal delivery decision.

### Initial packet

- Annual license costs for two tools; the third is missing.
- Estimated migration cost from an email.
- User-count screenshot with no date.
- AI-generated summary claiming “industry-standard consolidation saves 30%,” with a citation.
- Existing board template.

### Hidden facts

- The AI citation does not exist.
- The missing license cost is material to payback.
- Migration cost excludes internal labor.
- The board needs to decide whether to authorize discovery, not approve consolidation.
- The Lead reviewer becomes unavailable from 4:45–5:15 p.m. but can review at 5:15.
- The client will accept a discovery-decision exhibit if the strategist explains why.

### Timed events

1. **+5 minutes:** Client asks whether 30% savings is “safe to use as a benchmark.”
2. **+12 minutes:** Contributor reports the citation cannot be found.
3. **+20 minutes:** Client reveals the actual board decision if the candidate confirms it.
4. **+30 minutes:** Reviewer availability changes.

### Evidence expected

- Reframed decision and narrower artifact.
- Task assignment within Contributor authorization.
- False citation excluded and incident recorded.
- Assumptions and missing costs made visible.
- Review moved, not skipped.
- Client update with choices and recommendation.
- Internal delivery decision supported by Quality Review.

### Critical failure triggers

- Candidate uses the 30% claim after failed verification.
- Candidate promises twelve-month payback without the missing material inputs.
- Candidate delivers without required Lead approval.
- Candidate enters client data into an unapproved AI tool.

### Debrief questions

1. What changed when the actual board decision became clear?
2. Where did you narrow scope?
3. What intervention did the Contributor require?
4. What remains useful after the meeting?

## Simulation 4 — Lead Strategist crucible

### Competencies

All, with emphasis on C7 Client practice, C8 AI stewardship, C9 Quality assurance, and C10 Retainer leadership.

### Candidate brief

You are the accountable Lead Strategist for a pilot Executive Retainer. At the start of a 45-minute working period, you receive the queue below. Manage the team, make delivery decisions, communicate with the client, and explain your reasoning to the assessment panel.

### Starting queue

1. Board scenario requested today by the client's Chief of Staff.
2. Benchmark update due tomorrow; source definitions changed.
3. Agency pitch example drafted by a Supervised Strategist and scheduled for client review in two hours.
4. Quarterly “State of the Example” briefing due next week.

### Team

- One Contributor with strong research skills.
- One Supervised Strategist who authored the pitch example.
- One specialist reviewer available for ten minutes.

### Hidden defects and events

- The pitch example contains an AI-generated quote attributed to a plausible customer title.
- The benchmark compares two definitions as if they were identical.
- The board scenario uses sensitive workforce data pasted into an unapproved AI chat by the Contributor.
- The Chief of Staff asks to keep the problem “inside the team” and preserve today's delivery.
- The specialist reviewer flags a visually subtle denominator change.
- The Supervised Strategist becomes defensive after critical feedback.
- The quarterly briefing can be safely deferred with early notice.

### Event sequence

Facilitators release events based on the candidate's actions:

- If the candidate reviews by stakeholder seniority alone, reveal the sensitive-data incident late.
- If the candidate establishes a risk scan, reveal it immediately.
- If the candidate silently repairs the pitch, ask the Supervised Strategist to explain the final logic.
- If the candidate coaches and records ownership, the strategist proposes a valid correction.
- If the candidate preserves today's board delivery, introduce a question about whether the exposed data can remain in the working environment.
- If the candidate pauses appropriately, the client accepts a verbal decision discussion while remediation occurs.

### Evidence expected

- Immediate containment and escalation of the data incident.
- AI-generated quote removed and critical failure handled.
- Benchmark paused until definitions are reconciled or visibly separated.
- Work allocated within certification rights.
- Client communication that is timely, factual, and does not conceal the incident.
- Coaching that observes learner readiness instead of hiding rescue work.
- Retainer queue re-prioritized with rationale.
- Quality and delivery decisions recorded.
- Retrospective includes system changes, not only individual blame.

### Critical failure triggers

- Candidate conceals or continues using exposed client data.
- Candidate retains the invented quote or comparison after discovering the defects.
- Candidate bypasses review or pressures a subordinate to accept responsibility for the Lead's decision.
- Candidate retaliates against the person raising the issue.

### Panel transfer questions

1. Which decision would change if the board meeting moved by one week?
2. What part of the incident is a training defect versus an operating-system defect?
3. What client communication is required now, and who owns it?
4. Which team member remains authorized for which next action?
5. What evidence would you need before restoring paused work?

## Simulation scoring record

For every simulation, assessors record:

- Decisions and statements directly observed.
- Artifact evidence produced.
- Prompts or corrections required.
- Critical-failure review.
- Relevant competency ratings.
- One next-practice recommendation.
- Whether conditions were valid and accommodations functioned.
