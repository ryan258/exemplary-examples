# Facilitator Guide

## Facilitator mandate

The facilitator creates the conditions for learners to demonstrate judgment. They do not lecture learners into apparent agreement, reward consulting mannerisms, or rescue weak reasoning before it can be observed.

Facilitators must:

- Teach the published standard rather than personal preference.
- Separate practice feedback from certification decisions.
- Make accommodations routine and private.
- Use realistic pressure without humiliation, trickery, or inaccessible speed tests.
- Intervene immediately when practice risks confidential data, external publication, or real-world misrepresentation.
- Record curriculum defects instead of compensating for them invisibly.

## Before the cohort

- Confirm the curriculum version.
- Name the program owner, facilitators, assessors, and Lead supervisors.
- Review conflicts of interest.
- Collect participation and accessibility needs through a private channel.
- Prepare accessible source files, captions/transcripts, readable templates, and equivalent activity formats.
- Create fictional practice packets and scan them for accidental real-client information.
- Calibrate assessors using one sample artifact.
- Publish session purpose, prerequisites, evidence produced, and expected preparation.

## Learning environment

### Psychological safety with professional consequence

Learners must be able to expose uncertainty and revise work without losing dignity. At the same time, the facilitator must state clearly when a behavior would be unsafe in client work.

Use language such as:

- “This artifact is not yet ready because the audience could mistake the scenario for a real case.”
- “Show us the evidence behind that sentence.”
- “Which part is observed, calculated, or inferred?”
- “What would you change if the deadline were fixed but the review could not be skipped?”

Avoid:

- “You should already know this.”
- “That is not how consultants talk.”
- Feedback based on confidence, speed, eye contact, or verbal fluency when those are not the competency.
- Surprise assessment conditions unrelated to client reality.

## Standard session pattern

Use this structure for workshops:

1. **Orient (10%):** decision, competency, activity, evidence, standard.
2. **Demonstrate (15%):** facilitator works one compact example aloud.
3. **Practice (45%):** learners perform; facilitator observes rather than takes over.
4. **Critique (20%):** evidence-based peer and facilitator feedback.
5. **Transfer (10%):** learner states how the method changes their next artifact.

Publish breaks in advance. For longer sessions, schedule rather than merely permit them.

## Workshop guidance

### Workshop 1 — The decision beneath the request

**Preparation:** vague request cards, stakeholder constraints, Example Brief template.

**Watch for:** jumping directly to examples, treating the preferred recommendation as the decision, failing to ask what could change the answer, and accepting an artificial deadline without identifying the actual meeting.

**Debrief:** compare how different questions changed the scope. Ask which question produced the most decision value.

### Workshop 2 — Evidence under pressure

**Preparation:** mixed evidence packet with a known answer key and planted defects.

**Watch for:** source prestige replacing fitness, client assertions being silently upgraded, search snippets treated as sources, and AI citations accepted because they look plausible.

**Debrief:** have learners state not only what they rejected but what honest alternative the gap creates.

### Workshop 3 — Scenario architecture studio

**Preparation:** one decision brief, baseline dataset, architecture cards.

**Watch for:** choosing a sourced analogue by default, using realistic detail to mask missing support, and building only the preferred scenario.

**Debrief:** compare what each architecture allows the audience to conclude.

### Workshop 4 — Executive exhibit critique

**Preparation:** display method that remains accessible at zoom, critique form, timing alternatives.

**Watch for:** aesthetic preference disguised as quality, source notes used to excuse misleading headlines, and reviewers redesigning instead of diagnosing.

**Debrief:** require the artifact owner to state which feedback they accepted, rejected, and why.

## Simulation design

### Roles

- Candidate strategist.
- Client or stakeholder actor.
- Contributor actor when team leadership is assessed.
- Observer/assessor.
- Accessibility support role when requested.

One person may hold multiple non-conflicting roles, but the assessor should not act as the only stakeholder in a high-stakes certification simulation.

### Branching principle

Simulations should respond to the learner's choices. If the learner clarifies the decision, reveal the relevant constraint. If they promise a conclusion, increase conflicting evidence. If they establish a safe boundary, allow the client to engage with the alternative rather than forcing endless conflict.

The goal is to observe judgment, not to trap every candidate.

### Required simulation moments

Across the pathway, observe the learner:

- Clarify an ambiguous request.
- Discover a material contradiction.
- Explain a truth classification to a client.
- Narrow urgent scope without skipping critical review.
- Respond to pressure to conceal a scenario or invented detail.
- Correct an AI-generated error.
- Communicate a finding that does not support the client's preferred answer.
- Close with ownership, next action, and decision needed.

### Simulation reset

Stop and reset when:

- The learner or actor experiences an accessibility barrier not intrinsic to the competency.
- An actor introduces facts outside the scenario brief that materially change the answer.
- Technology failure prevents observation.
- The exercise moves into personal attack, identity-based pressure, or irrelevant distress.

A reset is not an automatic failure. Score only observable evidence produced under valid conditions.

## Feedback protocol

Use **Evidence → Effect → Standard → Next practice**:

1. **Evidence:** quote or describe the observed behavior/artifact.
2. **Effect:** explain the decision, client, or audience consequence.
3. **Standard:** cite the rubric or operating rule.
4. **Next practice:** prescribe a bounded opportunity to demonstrate improvement.

Example:

> The headline says the change “will improve margin,” while the body is a scenario with two unverified adoption assumptions. A reader could interpret a conditional result as a forecast. The Evidence Discipline and Exhibit Craft rubrics require certainty to match support. Rewrite three headline options using conditional language, then test each with a reader who sees only the slide.

## Knowledge-check guidance

Knowledge checks confirm the learner can recognize the standard. Accept equivalent wording when the reasoning is correct.

### Module 0 key

1. Prior experience may reduce instruction, but required observed simulations cannot be skipped.
2. Contributors may research, draft, and check work under review; they may not own commitments or deliver unreviewed work.
3. Accommodations remove irrelevant barriers while the competency standard remains unchanged.

### Module 1 key

1. No. Attribute and classify the claim; verification may still be needed for the intended use.
2. A modeled output does not become a sourced fact. If later observed, the observation is a separate sourced or client-provided fact.
3. Examples: specific invented company names, attributed quotes, logos, false citations, hidden scenario labels, or third-party research styling.
4. A composite combines disclosed case features for professional reasoning; satirical fiction is invented creative content confined to a satirical context.

### Module 2 key

1. It identifies a deliverable but not owner, choice, timing, alternatives, or use.
2. Reduce breadth, artifact size, or certainty; do not remove truth classification or critical review.
3. Whether the work can actually learn or is constrained to justify a predetermined answer.

### Module 3 key

1. Primary status is relative to the claim: an original company filing is primary for what the company reported, not necessarily for whether the report is true.
2. Speaker/author, source, date, context, and a retrieval path.
3. As an unverified research lead until the exact source is independently retrieved and checked.
4. When the search scope and method are defined and the absence is reported as a search result, not universal proof.

### Module 4 key

1. When no single case represents the relevant pattern and the combination can be disclosed clearly.
2. Transferability depends on the mechanism and material operating conditions, not category alone.
3. Which assumptions materially change the output or implication.
4. Labels such as “expected” or probability percentages without evidence establishing likelihood.

### Module 5 key

1. It names a subject but makes no decision-useful supported claim.
2. Whenever an audience could otherwise interpret conditional output as observed fact or forecast.
3. A concise note points to support; the full evidence register and working papers preserve the chain.
4. Examples: truncated axes, inconsistent denominators, area/volume encoding, selective time windows, or suppressed baselines.

### Module 6 key

1. Scope, breadth, or claimed certainty.
2. Decision, audience, deadline/meeting, owner, reviewer, service class, artifact, risk, or next checkpoint; any four.
3. When pressure persists, review is bypassed, authorization is exceeded, or material harm is likely.
4. Priority must reflect consequence, real deadline, dependency, readiness, and risk.

### Module 7 key

1. No. Unsupported claims and altered meaning can exist without citations.
2. Approved-tool policy matched to the classification of the actual data entered.
3. Tool/model/date, purpose, data class, material instruction, output treatment, verification, and reviewer.
4. Good: alternatives or rubric critique. AI may not own verification, delivery, or accountable judgment.

### Module 8 key

1. Scope, client communication, task authorization, review design, truth treatment, and final approval.
2. Consequence, novelty, sensitive data, urgent timing, inexperienced staff, new models, and travel beyond narration.
3. It hides true learner readiness and prevents system improvement.
4. When evidence shows the defect is recurrent rather than isolated.

## Facilitator observation log

After each session, record:

- Attendance and accommodation delivery without medical detail.
- Evidence produced.
- Common misconception.
- Curriculum instruction that caused confusion.
- Learners requiring specific next practice.
- Safety or policy issue and escalation.
- Change proposed for the next curriculum version.

Do not change a certification criterion informally during delivery. Flag it to the program owner.

## Facilitator readiness

A facilitator is ready when they have:

- Completed or demonstrated the course's core truth and AI standards.
- Observed one full workshop and co-facilitated one.
- Scored a calibration artifact within the agreed tolerance.
- Demonstrated the feedback protocol.
- Reviewed accessibility practices and simulation reset rules.
- Agreed to disclose conflicts and protect learner/client information.
