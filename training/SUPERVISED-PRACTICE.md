# Supervised Practice Guide

## Purpose

Supervised practice is the bridge between simulation and independent retainer ownership. It gives clients accountable service while making the learner's real performance observable.

Supervised practice is not inexpensive staffing and does not shift teaching risk onto a client. A named Lead Strategist remains accountable for scope, review, client communication, and delivery.

## Roles

### Learner

- Works only within the assigned scope and current authorization.
- Maintains working papers, decision records, and status.
- Raises uncertainty early.
- Does not send external work without the required approval.
- Reflects on feedback and produces revised evidence.

### Lead supervisor

- Confirms that the assignment is suitable for learning and client safety.
- Defines authorization, review gates, and escalation.
- Observes enough of the work to assess actual performance.
- Reviews before external delivery.
- Distinguishes learner work from supervisor rescue work.
- Gives evidence-based feedback and makes the readiness recommendation.

### Client owner

The person accountable for the commercial relationship. Often the Lead supervisor, but if separate, both agree who communicates scope changes and delivery decisions.

### Program owner

Monitors placement quality, supervisor capacity, critical incidents, and certification evidence.

## Placement requirements

Before placement:

- Learner holds active Contributor certification.
- Learner has passed required simulations for the assigned activity.
- Client owner approves learner participation and communication role.
- Confidentiality, data location, and AI tool rules are documented.
- Assignment has a defined decision, artifact, deadline, reviewer, and learning objective.
- Lead supervisor has capacity to review without relying on late rescue.
- Accessibility arrangements work in the real delivery environment.

## Progression stages

### Stage 1 — Observe and reconstruct

The learner observes an intake or review, then independently reconstructs the decision brief or quality assessment. The Lead compares reasoning afterward.

**May:** take notes in approved locations, ask questions after agreed moments, draft internal artifacts.  
**May not:** lead the client interaction or make commitments.

**Exit evidence:** accurate reconstruction that identifies the material decision, uncertainty, and next action.

### Stage 2 — Own an internal work product

The learner owns a bounded internal artifact such as an Evidence Register, Example Blueprint, model, or draft exhibit.

**May:** coordinate approved Contributor work and present internally.  
**May not:** send the artifact externally or change client scope.

**Exit evidence:** artifact reaches internal delivery standard without the Lead replacing the core reasoning.

### Stage 3 — Lead a bounded client moment

The learner leads part of intake, a working session, or an artifact walkthrough. The Lead is present or immediately available as agreed.

**May:** ask questions, explain method, present approved work, and confirm next steps within scope.  
**May not:** approve final delivery, accept an exception, or commit work outside the brief.

**Exit evidence:** observed client-practice rubric at Ready with accurate follow-up.

### Stage 4 — Manage a request cycle

The learner manages one request from brief through retrospective under explicit Lead gates.

**May:** coordinate workflow, recommend scope and priority, conduct working sessions, and prepare delivery.  
**May not:** waive gates or send final work without approval.

**Exit evidence:** complete Portfolio B-quality engagement record and supervisor evaluation.

### Stage 5 — Manage a retainer segment

For Lead candidates, the learner manages a meaningful portion of the queue across multiple requests, including prioritization, team allocation, client communication, and review.

**Exit evidence:** two sustained engagement cycles, including at least one adverse event or material change handled safely.

## Required review gates

| Gate | Required evidence | Approver |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Brief approval | Decision, audience, claim, scope, deadline, evidence needs, constraints | Lead Strategist |
| Evidence/model approval | Evidence Register, working calculations, assumptions, gaps | Lead or assigned qualified reviewer |
| Draft review | Exhibit/package and six-angle Quality Review | Lead Strategist |
| Delivery approval | Final files, truth markers, source/method notes, delivery context | Lead Strategist |
| Retrospective | Outcome, feedback, defects, process improvement, learner reflection | Learner + Lead |

Urgency may combine meetings but may not eliminate the evidence/model or delivery approval decisions.

## Supervisor observation

The supervisor records what they directly observed, what the artifact demonstrates, what prompting was required, and what they changed themselves.

Classify supervisor intervention:

- **Clarification:** restated the task or standard.
- **Prompt:** directed attention to a problem without supplying the solution.
- **Coaching:** discussed options and reasoning.
- **Correction:** supplied a material fix.
- **Rescue:** took over ownership to protect delivery.

A deliverable may succeed while the learner is not yet ready. Repeated correction or any rescue must be visible in the readiness decision.

## Check-in cadence

At minimum:

- **Assignment start:** scope, role, learning objective, review gates.
- **Early check:** learner restates decision and work plan before significant production.
- **Pre-delivery:** quality and authorization decision.
- **Post-delivery:** client response, defects, learning, next practice.
- **Weekly during active placement:** workload, supervision capacity, accessibility, and emerging risk.

## Stop-work and escalation

The learner stops and contacts the Lead when:

- A client requests undisclosed fictional, composite, or modeled material.
- A material source cannot be verified.
- Sensitive data appears outside the approved system.
- A calculation or truth marker changes after approval.
- Deadline pressure threatens a required review.
- The learner is asked to act beyond authorization.
- A personal, accessibility, or safety condition prevents reliable work.

Stopping is a professional control, not a failure. Concealing the issue is a performance concern.

## Client communication

The client should know who owns the relationship and, where relevant, that another team member is working under that person's review. Do not describe a learner in diminishing terms or disclose disability/accommodation information.

Suggested introduction:

> Alex will lead today's working session and manage this request with me as the accountable Lead Strategist. I will review the evidence treatment and approve the final delivery.

## Completing an engagement cycle

The cycle counts toward Lead evidence when:

- It involved a real client decision or a program-owner-approved equivalent.
- The learner owned meaningful work across framing, production, communication, and review.
- The supervisor observed enough behavior to score the relevant competencies.
- Working papers and approvals are complete.
- Any rescue work is disclosed.
- The learner and supervisor independently reflect before comparing conclusions.

## Supervisor recommendation

At the end of a placement, recommend one:

- **Advance to assessment:** evidence consistently meets the target level.
- **Continue at current stage:** more evidence is needed; name the experience required.
- **Narrow practice:** a specific risk requires reduced scope and remediation.
- **Pause placement:** client safety, conduct, accessibility failure, or supervisor capacity prevents valid practice.

The supervisor recommends; the certification panel decides.
