# Interview Guide Friction Log (Process Critique)

This is the primary deliverable of the synthetic dry run. It critiques the **interview guide itself**
(`discovery/INTERVIEW-GUIDE.md`), based on running six fictional personas through it. It says nothing
reliable about the market — only about how the instrument behaved.

**Method caveat:** Synthetic personas are cooperative and articulate in ways real participants are not,
so this log likely *understates* friction. Where a real interview would stall, a synthetic one glides.
Read every "under-elicited" note as "at least this bad, probably worse in the field."

## What worked

- **Behavior-before-opinion ordering (Parts 1–3 before 4–6)** did its job: it forced concrete recent
  stories (P1's board deck, P3's dispute, P5's reorg memo) before the brand could bias them.
- **The refusal-conditions question (Q18)** surfaced the sharpest, most specific objections in the set
  (P3's named-accountability demand, P5's AI-credibility block). Keep it.
- **Separating "would trust" from "would introduce" (Q21 vs Q22)** cleanly caught conditional buyers who
  personally liked EE but would not attach it to a client (P2).
- **The design-partner rubric (Part 7)** correctly disqualified warmth-without-ownership (P4 = general only)
  and no-need (P6 = no fit). The "general interest does not count" rule earned its place.

## Questions that OVER-elicited (produce soft/duplicative signal)

| Q | Problem | Suggested change |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Q14 "immediate reaction" to the truth standard | Everyone reacts positively — it's a virtuous-sounding statement. Near-zero discriminating power. | Cut or fold into Q15; ask instead "which part would you *remove* to ship faster?" to force a tradeoff. |
| Q19 "What do you think this company does?" | Useful once for framing-failure detection, but overlaps heavily with Q20; personas answered both nearly identically. | Merge Q19 into Q20; keep the misunderstanding capture, drop the duplication. |
| Q25 "useful or unnecessary" parts of the offer | Too broad; produced vague approval. | Split into "what would you delete" and "what's missing" — deletions are more honest than approvals. |
| Q23 (most/least trust phrase) | Overlaps with Q15 and Q20; three questions circle the same "which words land" ground. | Consolidate the language-testing into one place. |

## Questions that UNDER-elicited (missed signal they should have caught)

| Gap | What was missed | Suggested change |
| --- | --- | --- |
| No question isolates **who the buyer actually is vs. who owns the problem**. | P4 (presentation consultant) sailed through Parts 1–3 sounding qualified, but *owns craft, not the argument*. The guide only caught this at Part 7. | Add an early Part-1 screen: "When an example is wrong, who gets the call — you, or someone upstream?" Ownership ≠ involvement. |
| Nothing probes the **AI-staffing credibility** reaction before Part 6. | P5's decisive objection (AI-authored analysis lacks credibility for sensitive work) only appeared at Q part-6, after 25+ minutes. | Add a targeted probe: "If a disclosed AI agent produced this under a named human's sign-off, does that help or hurt with your audience?" |
| No question asks whether the **satire near their brand/procurement** is itself a risk. | P2, P3, P6 all raised brand-adjacency/optics risk unprompted — the single most repeated negative. The guide has no home for it. | Add to Part 5: "Where would this brand appearing publicly create a problem for you?" |
| Q29 (budget) is easy to dodge. | Personas gave a category ("a few hundred a month") but the guide's own caveat ("do not force a price") let softer personas skip it entirely (P2, P6). | Keep the caveat, but add a behavioral anchor: "What do you spend today on the closest substitute?" — grounds it in actual behavior. |

## Ordering problems

1. **The satire reveal (Part 5) lands the fabrication misread repeatedly, and the guide has no recovery move.**
   P2/P3/P6 all first read "we build your perfect example" as fabrication-as-a-service. The guide correctly
   says "do not explain away the misunderstanding" — good for evidence — but there is no *follow-up question*
   to learn whether the misread survives the truth-standard context or is curable. Add one neutral probe after
   the reveal: "Now that you've heard the disclosure standard, has your read of the tagline changed?" (Record
   both readings; do not argue.)

2. **Accountability (the accountable principal) is introduced too late (Part 6, Q94-region).** It was the
   single strongest trust-builder across P1/P3/P5, yet it arrives *after* the AI-staff framing has already
   damaged credibility. Consider surfacing "an accountable human principal stands behind every claim" during
   the Part 4 truth-standard framing, before the AI-staff language in Part 6.

3. **Part 7 comes too late to shape the conversation.** By the time design-partner fit is assessed, the
   moderator has spent 30 minutes on personas (P4, P6) who were never going to fit. An early ownership +
   recent-frequency gate (see under-elicited table) would let the moderator down-scope a clearly-no-fit
   session and reallocate time.

## Additions to consider

- A one-line **early disqualifier**: recent frequency of the *specific* behavior (not "do you use examples,"
  but "when did you last have to *find or build* a comparable that didn't exist"). P6 would have screened out
  in two minutes.
- A **two-reading capture** for the tagline (pre- and post-truth-standard), since the misread is the central
  framing risk.
- A **brand-adjacency risk** question (above), because for agencies and procurement the brand's *visibility*,
  not its *ethics*, is the blocker.

## Cuts to consider

- Collapse Q14/Q19/Q23 into fewer, sharper language-and-reaction questions (they triangulate the same thing).
- Drop or rewrite Q25 into deletion/gap form.

## Top 3 guide improvements (ranked)

1. **Add an early problem-ownership + recent-frequency gate in Part 1.** The guide's biggest failure was
   letting a downstream supplier (P4) and a no-need buyer (P6) run to Part 7 before disqualifying. Catch
   ownership and specific recent frequency in the first five minutes.
2. **Move accountable-human-principal framing earlier, and add an AI-staffing credibility probe.** The most
   decision-relevant objection (AI-authored analysis lacks credibility for sensitive work) surfaced too late
   and had no dedicated question. Front-load human accountability; probe the AI reaction directly.
3. **Add a tagline two-reading capture + a brand-adjacency risk question in Part 5.** The fabrication misread
   recurred (P2/P3/P6) and the guide had no way to test whether it's curable or where the brand's mere
   visibility is the liability. Capture the misread before and after the truth-standard context, and ask
   where the public brand would create a problem *for them*.

## Reminder

The value of this exercise is this critique, not the personas' answers. A synthetic interview cannot tell
you whether the market wants this — only whether your instrument is sharp enough to find out when real
people are in the room.
