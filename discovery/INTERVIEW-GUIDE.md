# Customer Discovery Interview Guide

## Session parameters

**Length:** 30–40 minutes  
**Mode:** Conversation; written responses are an equivalent option  
**Researcher:**  
**Date:**  

## Moderator principles

- Ask about recent behavior before opinions about the idea.
- Request specific examples without collecting confidential client information.
- Do not defend the brand when a participant reacts negatively.
- Separate “interesting” from willingness to change behavior, introduce the service, or join a pilot.
- Record exact phrases sparingly and only with permission; otherwise paraphrase accurately.
- Do not ask disability, protected-class, or sensitive personal questions unrelated to participation access.

## Opening

> We are researching how consultants and presentation professionals find, model, and communicate examples when preparing business cases. This is research, not a sales call. Please avoid sharing client-confidential names, materials, or data. I will take written notes. You may skip any question or stop at any time.

Confirm:

- [ ] Participant agrees to continue.
- [ ] Recording is off unless separately and explicitly approved.
- [ ] Participant knows not to disclose confidential client information.

## Part 1 — Role and context

1. What kind of work do you do, and where do examples or case comparisons appear in it?
2. Who usually requests that work, who produces it, and who approves it?
3. How often have you handled this kind of request in the last three months?

**Listen for:** actual frequency, buying influence, delivery responsibility, and whether the participant is inside the target workflow.

**Early fit screen (apply before continuing):**

- **Ownership:** "When an example turns out to be wrong or gets challenged, who gets the call—you, or someone upstream?" Owning the argument is the signal; owning only the craft or format is not.
- **Specific recent frequency:** "When did you last have to *find or build* a comparable or example that did not already exist?" A vague answer, or one older than six months, is a down-scope signal—continue at full depth only when the participant owns the problem and shows recent, repeated behavior.

## Part 2 — Most recent behavior

4. Think about the most recent time someone asked, “Has anyone else done this?” or wanted a supporting example. What happened from request to delivery?
5. What made that request difficult?
6. Where did you look, what tools or people did you use, and how long did it take?
7. What did you deliver when no real case fit well?
8. What did the audience believe the example represented?
9. Was anything left unresolved, softened, or removed because the evidence did not fit?

**Probe without leading:**

- What was the deadline tied to?
- What other work was displaced?
- Who checked sources, calculations, or assumptions?
- Did the deliverable travel beyond the original meeting?
- What would have happened if nothing were delivered?

## Part 3 — Cost and current alternatives

10. What does the current process cost in time, quality, risk, or opportunity?
11. What do you do today to make the process faster or safer?
12. What is good enough about the current approach that would make you resist changing it?
13. If you could improve only one part of the process, which part would matter most?

**Evidence strength:** recent repeated behavior is stronger than generalized frustration; allocated budget or displaced work is stronger than praise.

## Part 4 — Truth and disclosure boundary

Introduce the professional standard without the brand pitch:

> One possible service would help teams build sourced examples, disclosed composites, and transparent modeled scenarios. It would explicitly distinguish sourced facts, client-provided facts, calculations, inferences, and modeled scenarios. Fictional material would never be presented as a verified real case in professional work. A named, accountable human principal stands behind every professional claim; any AI assistance supports that accountability but never replaces it.

14. What is your immediate reaction?
15. Which part increases trust? Which part creates friction?
16. How would your client, managing partner, legal team, or executive audience react to a modeled or composite example?
17. What labeling would be credible without making the work unusable?
18. Under what conditions would you refuse to use this service?

## Part 5 — Reveal the satirical brand

Show the prototype or a concise brand statement:

> Exemplary Examples™ is an openly satirical brand built around the line: “Every business case needs one perfect example. We build yours.” Behind the satire is a real professional practice using the truth standard we just discussed.

19. What do you think this company does?
20. What feels insightful, irresponsible, confusing, or memorable?
21. Would you trust it with a client-sensitive assignment? Why or why not?
22. Would you introduce it to a client or managing partner? What would you need to see first?
23. Which phrase creates the most trust? Which phrase loses the most trust?
24. Explain the professional disclosure boundary back in your own words.

**Follow-ups (record both, do not argue):**

- **Tagline re-read:** "Now that you have heard the disclosure standard, has your read of the line 'we build your perfect example' changed?" Capture the before and after reading; a misread that survives the standard is a different signal from one the standard cures.
- **Brand-adjacency risk:** "Where would this brand appearing publicly create a problem for you or your clients?" For some buyers the brand's *visibility*, not its ethics, is the blocker.

Do not explain away a misunderstanding. Record it as evidence that the framing failed.

## Part 6 — Offer and buying process

Describe the concept without promising unproven service levels:

> The initial concept is a limited, application-only Executive Retainer pilot: disclosed AI staff agents, governed by an accountable principal and documented review systems, help with recurring example and exhibit requests. The pilot is designed to prove the operating process before broader launch.

**AI-staffing credibility probe:** If a disclosed AI agent produced this work under a named human principal's sign-off, does that help or hurt with your audience—and why?

25. If you kept this offer, what would you delete first, and what is missing that you would need?
26. Would recurring access solve the problem better than a one-time project?
27. What turnaround expectations would be meaningful and credible?
28. Who would approve participation, and what evidence or terms would they require?
29. What budget would this compete with, and what do you spend today on the closest substitute? Do not force a price estimate if the participant does not buy similar services.
30. What would cause a pilot to fail even if the deliverables looked good?

## Part 7 — Design-partner signal

Ask only when the participant has described a real problem and plausible fit:

> We expect to invite two or three design partners to test the process under clearly defined terms. Would you be willing to consider a follow-up conversation once the pilot scope and protections are ready?

Record one:

- **Concrete follow-up:** participant agrees to a named next action or introduction.
- **Conditional interest:** participant names a specific condition that must be met.
- **General interest:** positive words without commitment.
- **No fit:** declines or lacks the relevant need/authority.

General interest does not count toward the two-to-three design-partner target.

## Close

31. What should I have asked but did not?
32. Who else sees this problem differently and would be useful to interview?

> Thank you. I will use the findings to decide whether and how this service should proceed. I will not attribute your comments publicly without separate permission.
