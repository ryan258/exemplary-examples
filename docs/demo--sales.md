# Demo — Sales Walkthrough

A stage-by-stage script for walking a prospect through Exemplary Examples™ and winning the relationship. The differentiator is **trust, not hype**: this brand builds the perfect example *and* shows its work, so a prospect can put it in front of their board without fear.

## The one idea to land

> "Everyone can make a slide look convincing. We make an example your board can *trust* — because we label exactly what's sourced, what's calculated, and what's modeled. You get persuasion you can defend under cross-examination."

If the prospect leaves remembering one thing, it's that: **defensible persuasion.**

## Know before you walk in

- The prospect's **actual pending decision** (the one an example would support). If you don't know it, the demo's first job is to find it.
- Their **audience** (board? client? regulator?) and what would make that audience distrust an exhibit.
- Have the site open to [Home](../index.html) and be ready to jump to the **Method** section.
- 15–20 minutes. This is a conversation with a live artifact, not a pitch deck.

## The script

### 0. Disarm the satire (60 seconds)

Get the joke out of the way, then pivot hard to the real practice.

- **Say:** "The brand is an open joke — 'every business case needs one perfect example, we build yours.' The joke points at a real bad habit: manufacturing evidence *after* the decision's already made. The company exists to do the opposite of what it satirizes."
- **Show:** the disclosure line and the About panel — *"The joke is recognizable. The boundary is real."*
- **Goal:** they laugh, they get it, and they stop wondering whether you're serious. Now you're serious.

### 1. Diagnose the real pain (3–4 min)

Make them describe the problem in their own words before you sell anything.

- **Ask:** "Think about the last time someone asked *'has anyone else done this?'* right before a big decision. What did you actually deliver when no real case fit cleanly?"
- **Listen for:** the scramble, the softened claim, the exhibit that traveled further than intended, the reviewer who didn't check the sources.
- **Goal:** they say the pain out loud. You're now solving *their* problem, not demoing a product.

### 2. The reveal — method, not mythology (4–5 min)

This is the differentiator. Slow down here.

- **Show:** the **Method** section — Frame → Classify → Construct → Challenge → Deliver → Learn — and the **six-class truth standard** (Sourced fact · Client-provided fact · Calculation · Inference · Modeled scenario · Satirical fiction).
- **Say:** "Every material claim in what we hand you carries one of these labels. Your CFO can point at any number and ask 'where's this from?' and there's an answer. That's the product."
- **Point at the Proof section:** "Notice there are no client logos or invented win-rate stats here. We refuse to fake proof about ourselves — which is exactly why you can trust what we build for you."
- **Goal:** the reframe lands — this isn't a prettier deck, it's *accountable evidence*.

### 3. Walk a worked example (3–4 min)

Show the method on something concrete — ideally adjacent to their decision.

- Take a small, honest example and narrate the labels out loud: "This figure is **sourced** — here's the citation and access date. This projection is a **modeled scenario** — here are the assumptions and the sensitivity. This is an **inference**, and here's the reasoning."
- If you build live, build small and label as you go. **Never** dress a modeled number as a sourced fact to make the moment land harder — that's the one move that loses the whole sale later.
- **Goal:** they *see* trust being constructed, not asserted.

### 4. The pivotal objection — "so you fabricate evidence?" (2–3 min)

This will come. It's not a threat; it's your best moment. Welcome it.

- **Say:** "Fair question — and it's the whole point. Satirical products can be pure fiction. Professional work never passes fiction, composites, or models off as a real case. If a reasonable audience could mistake something we built for a verified real-world fact, we change the labeling or the artifact *before* it ships. That's a rule, not a preference."
- **Flip it:** "The firms that *should* worry you are the ones whose examples all look equally certain. We make the seams visible on purpose."
- **Goal:** the objection becomes the reason to hire you.

### 5. The offer (2 min)

Frame it small and disciplined on purpose — scarcity of rigor, not scarcity as a trick.

- **Say:** "The way in is an application-only **Executive Retainer pilot**. Disclosed specialists operate the workflow inside explicit permissions; a named, accountable human stands behind every claim we deliver. It's deliberately limited because the pilot's job is to prove the process on real work of yours — not to maximize headcount."
- **Be clear what it is *not*:** not an unlimited promise, not guaranteed acceptance, not a place your confidential data goes before the terms are set.
- **Goal:** the limits read as seriousness.

### 6. Close — the next step, not the signature (1–2 min)

Ask for a *specific, small* commitment tied to their real decision.

- **Ask:** "Would you bring us one live decision you're preparing for — under NDA — and let us show you a labeled example you could actually take into that room?"
- Record the outcome honestly: **concrete next step** (a named action/intro) / **conditional** (they name a condition) / **interested** (warm but no commitment) / **no fit**. Only the first two are real progress. Don't upgrade warmth into a win in your own notes — same discipline you just sold them.

## Objection cheat-sheet

| They say… | You land… |
| --- | --- |
| "The satire feels risky near my brand." | "Disclosed once, then straight-faced. The satire is the hook; the work your audience sees is sober and labeled. And where it'd be a problem for you, it simply doesn't appear." |
| "Isn't this just AI output?" | "AI helps produce and critique. A named human is accountable for every source, assumption, and delivery — and we keep the work log to prove it." |
| "Can I trust this in front of my board?" | "That's the design goal. Every claim is classified and traceable, so any challenge has an answer in the room." |
| "What does it cost?" | "The pilot is scoped to prove value on one real decision first. We'd rather earn the retainer than quote one." |

## Never (the fastest way to lose this sale for good)

- Present a modeled, composite, or fictional item as a verified real case, quote, customer, or result.
- Show invented testimonials or win-rate statistics as real proof.
- Let a prospect's urgency talk you out of labeling and disclosure.
- Promise acceptance, unlimited scope, or a service level the pilot hasn't proven.

## Leave-behind (one line)

> "We build the clearest example for the decision in front of you — and make its relationship to reality unmistakable, so you can defend it."
